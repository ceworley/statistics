First, you need to identify that we are looking for a central area. This is because \(z\)-scores near 0 satisfy \(|Z|<0.66\), while \(z\)-scores far from 0 (either positive or negative) do not satisfy the inequality.

Start with a sketch.


Remember, the entire area is always 1. Most z-tables only provide left areas, so three methods are shown to get the central area from a left-area (cumulative probability) table.

Method 1: First find the area of the left tail. \[P(Z<-0.66) = 0.2546\] Recognize normal distributions are symmetric, so we also know the area of the right tail. \[P(Z>0.66) = 0.2546 \] The three areas add to 1. \[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|<0.66\right) &= 1-P(Z<-0.66)-P(Z>0.66) \\ &= 1 - 0.2546 - 0.2546 \\ &= 0.4908 \end{aligned}\]

This technique can be summarized with the following formula: \[P\left(|Z|<z\right) = 1-2\cdot P(Z<-z)\] assuming \(z>0\).

So, in our case (when \(z=0.66\)), \[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|<0.66\right) &= 1-2\cdot P(Z<-0.66) \\ &= 1-2\cdot0.2546 \\ &= 0.4908 \end{aligned}\]

Method 2: You can also find half of the central area and then double it. \[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|<z\right) &= 2\cdot\left[ P(Z<z)-0.5\right] \\ &= 2\cdot\left[ 0.7454-0.5\right] \\ &= 2\cdot\left[ 0.2454\right] \\ &= 0.4908 \end{aligned}\]

This method is shown graphically:


Method 3: You can also calculate the central area with a difference of two left areas.

\[\begin{aligned} P\left(|Z|<0.66\right) &= P(Z<0.66)-P(Z<-0.66)\\ &= 0.7454-0.2546 \\ &= 0.4908 \end{aligned}\]
