The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a measurement is the ratio with numerator the difference between measurement and population mean and denominator the population standard deviation.

\[z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \]

The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large measurement.

The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small measurement.

The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized measurement.

The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized measurement.


  1. We use the formula for calculating the orange specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{200.6-194}{12} = 0.55 \]
  2. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.55) = 0.7088 \]
  3. We use the formula for calculating the pineapple specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{1722-1474}{130} = 1.91 \]
  4. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<1.91) = 0.9719 \]
  5. We use the formula for calculating the kiwi specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{64.84-66}{4} = -0.29 \]
  6. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-0.29) = 0.3859 \]
  7. We use the formula for calculating the persimmon specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{145-137}{11} = 0.73 \]
  8. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.73) = 0.7673 \]
