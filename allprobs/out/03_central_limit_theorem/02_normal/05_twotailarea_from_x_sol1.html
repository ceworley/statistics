First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(H\) axis by adding integer multiples of 16 to 51. We know to shade the two tails because values far from 51 satisfy the condition \(\big|H-51\big| > 16\). We draw the boundaries at \(51-16=35\) and \(51+16=67\) because those are the solutions to \(\big|H-51\big| = 16\). We can also rephrase the probability.

\[P\left(\big|H-51\big| > 16\right) ~=~ P\big(H<35 \text{ OR } H>67\big)\]


We calculate the \(z\) values of the boundaries. Left boundary: \[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{35-51}{16} \\\\ &= \frac{-16}{16} \\\\ &= -1 \end{aligned}\]

Right boundary:

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{67-51}{16} \\\\ &= \frac{16}{16} \\\\ &= 1 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because

\[P\left(\big|H-51\big| > 16\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big|>1\right)\]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(|Z|>1)\). I will also point out that \(\frac{16}{16} = 1\). In general, if \(X\) is normally distributed, then: \[P\left(\big|X-\mu\big|>d\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big| > \frac{d}{\sigma}\right)\]

From here we have a formula that lets us use the \(z\) table. (We practiced this part before.) \[\begin{aligned} P(|Z|>1) &= 2- 2 \times P(Z<1) \\ &= 2 - 2 \times 0.8413 \\ &= 0.3174 \end{aligned}\]
