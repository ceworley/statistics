The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a measurement is the ratio with numerator the difference between measurement and population mean and denominator the population standard deviation.

\[z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \]

The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large measurement.

The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small measurement.

The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized measurement.

The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized measurement.


  1. We use the formula for calculating the papaya specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{387.4-416}{24} = -1.19 \]
  2. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-1.19) = 0.117 \]
  3. We use the formula for calculating the kiwi specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{116.4-100}{10} = 1.64 \]
  4. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<1.64) = 0.9495 \]

  6. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<1.96) = 0.975 \]
  7. We use the formula for calculating the banana specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{198.8-203}{11} = -0.38 \]
  8. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-0.38) = 0.352 \]
