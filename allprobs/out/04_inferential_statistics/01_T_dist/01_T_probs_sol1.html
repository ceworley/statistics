You can use a \(t\) table. Remember, the degree of freedom (df or \(\nu\)) is one less than \(n\). \[\text{df} = n-1 \]

Spreadsheet

You can use T.DIST and T.INV to calculate the values. But remember these functions return/use LEFT-area probabilities.

R

You can use pt and qt to calculate the values. But remember these functions return/use LEFT-area probabilities.


  1. When \(n=34\), then \(\text{df}=33\) and \(P(T<1.31)=0.9\).
  2. When \(n=22\), then \(\text{df}=21\) and \(P(T>2.19)=0.02\).
  3. When \(n=22\), then \(\text{df}=21\) and \(P(|T|<1.32)=0.8\).
  4. When \(n=11\), then \(\text{df}=10\) and \(P(|T|>1.81)=0.1\).

  7. When \(n=16\), then \(\text{df}=15\) and \(P(|T|<2.95)=0.99\), so \(t=2.95\).
