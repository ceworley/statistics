The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (ANS=49) There are 49 measurements less than 3.5. So, \(\#[\mathbf{x}<3.5]=49\).
  2. (ANS=51) There are 51 measurements more than 3.5. So, \(\#[\mathbf{x}>3.5]=51\).
  3. (ANS=18) There are 18 measurements closer than 0.5 units from 2. So, \(\#[|\mathbf{x}-2|<0.5]=18\).
  4. (ANS=72) There are 72 measurements further than 1 units from 3.5. So, \(\#[|\mathbf{x}-3.5|>1]=72\).
  5. (ANS=2.5) A boundary at \(b=2.5\) has 36 measurements less than it. So, \(\#[\mathbf{x}<2.5]=36\).
  6. (ANS=4.5) A boundary at \(b=4.5\) has 36 measurements more than it. So, \(\#[\mathbf{x}>4.5]=36\).
  7. (ANS=0.5) An interval with radius \(r=0.5\) around center 5 contains 15 measurements. So, \(\#[|\mathbf{x}-5|<0.5]=15\).
  8. (ANS=1) An interval with radius \(r=1\) around center 2.5 excludes 69 measurements. So, \(\#[|\mathbf{x}-2.5|>1]=69\).
