You may find it helpful to convert the densities to frequencies by multiplying each density by both the total sample size (\(n=500\)) and the width of the bar (1).


The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (0.408) There are 204 measurements less than 52. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<52]=0.408\).
  2. (0.404) There are 202 measurements more than 53. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>53]=0.404\).
  3. (0.614) There are 307 measurements closer than 1.5 units from 52.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-52.5|<1.5]=0.614\).
  4. (0.798) There are 399 measurements further than 0.5 units from 51.5. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-51.5|>0.5]=0.798\).
  5. .(53) A boundary at \(b=53\) has 298 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<53]=0.596\).
  6. .(52) A boundary at \(b=52\) has 296 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>52]=0.592\).
  7. .(1) An interval with radius \(r=1\) around center 53 contains 206 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-53|<1]=0.412\).
  8. .(0.5) An interval with radius \(r=0.5\) around center 52.5 excludes 406 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-52.5|>0.5]=0.812\).
