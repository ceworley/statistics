We determine the mean and standard deviation of the binomial distribution. \[\mu ~=~ np ~=~ (59)(0.2) ~=~ 11.8\] \[\sigma ~=~ \sqrt{np(1-p)} ~=~ \sqrt{(59)(0.2)(0.8)} ~=~ 3.0724583 \]

We determine both \(z\)-scores. (In the de Moivre-Laplace notes, I used \(\sum x\) to emphasize that a binomial variable is a sum of Bernoulli trials. Here, I will just use \(x\) as the boundary for number of successes, because this is the more common notation.) We get the first \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{x_1 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{11.5-11.8}{3.0724583} \\\\ &= -0.0976417 \end{aligned}\]

We get the second \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{x_2 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{16.5-11.8}{3.0724583} \\\\ &= 1.5297197 \end{aligned}\]

Then, find the standard normal probability.

\[P(-0.0976417 < Z < 1.5297197) ~=~ 0.4758485 \]

Of course, you could have rounded the \(z\)-scores, so any of the following will also get credit. \[P(-0.1 < Z < 1.52) ~=~ 0.4755 \] \[P(-0.09 < Z < 1.53) ~=~ 0.4729 \] \[P(-0.1 < Z < 1.53) ~=~ 0.4716 \] \[P(-0.09 < Z < 1.52) ~=~ 0.4768 \]
