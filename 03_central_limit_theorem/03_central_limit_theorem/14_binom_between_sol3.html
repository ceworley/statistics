We determine the mean and standard deviation of the binomial distribution. \[\mu ~=~ np ~=~ (169)(0.33) ~=~ 55.77\] \[\sigma ~=~ \sqrt{np(1-p)} ~=~ \sqrt{(169)(0.33)(0.67)} ~=~ 6.1127653 \]

We determine both \(z\)-scores. (In the de Moivre-Laplace notes, I used \(\sum x\) to emphasize that a binomial variable is a sum of Bernoulli trials. Here, I will just use \(x\) as the boundary for number of successes, because this is the more common notation.) We get the first \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{x_1 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{50.5-55.77}{6.1127653} \\\\ &= -0.8621303 \end{aligned}\]

We get the second \(z\)-score.

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{x_2 - \mu}{\sigma} \\\\ &= \frac{60.5-55.77}{6.1127653} \\\\ &= 0.7737905 \end{aligned}\]

Then, find the standard normal probability.

\[P(-0.8621303 < Z < 0.7737905) ~=~ 0.5861647 \]

Of course, you could have rounded the \(z\)-scores, so any of the following will also get credit. \[P(-0.87 < Z < 0.77) ~=~ 0.5872 \] \[P(-0.86 < Z < 0.78) ~=~ 0.5874 \] \[P(-0.87 < Z < 0.78) ~=~ 0.5845 \] \[P(-0.86 < Z < 0.77) ~=~ 0.5901 \]
