A farm produces 4 types of fruit: papayas, bananas, watermelons, and persimmons. The fruits’ masses have population parameters dependent on the type of fruit. (All values are in grams.)

Type of fruit \(\mu\), population mean mass in grams \(\sigma\), population standard deviation in grams
papayas 561 92
bananas 205 10
watermelons 8524 476
persimmons 135 12

A sample of each type is weighed. The results are shown below.

Type of fruit \(n\), sample size \(\bar{x}\), sample mean
papaya 100 574.2
banana 25 205.0
watermelon 36 8546.0
persimmon 49 132.3

The population parameters and sample statistics can be downloaded as a csv.

For each sample, determine the mean’s standard score and the mean’s cumulative probability by assuming the requirements for the central limit theorem are met. Then determine which sample mean is most unusually large, most unusually small, most typically sized, and most unusually sized.

The standard score of a sample mean: \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\] Some people prefer to call this denominator the standard error. \[\text{standard error of mean} = \text{SEM} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \] So the standard score (\(z\)-score) of a sample mean can also be expressed as \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\text{SEM}} \]

  2. What proportion of papaya samples of size \(n=100\) have a smaller mean than the papaya sample?
  3. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the banana sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
