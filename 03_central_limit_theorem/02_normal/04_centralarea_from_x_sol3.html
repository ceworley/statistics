First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(I\) axis by adding integer multiples of 5 to 58. We know to shade the center because values near 58 satisfy the condition \(\big|I-58\big| < 2\). We draw the boundaries at \(58-2=56\) and \(58+2=60\) because those are the solutions to \(\big|I-58\big| = 2\). We can also rephrase the probability.

\[P\left(\big|I-58\big| < 2\right) ~=~ P(56<I<60)\]


We calculate the \(z\) values of the boundaries. Left boundary: \[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{56-58}{5} \\\\ &= \frac{-2}{5} \\\\ &= -0.4 \end{aligned}\]

Right boundary:

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{60-58}{5} \\\\ &= \frac{2}{5} \\\\ &= 0.4 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because

\[P\left(\big|I-58\big| < 2\right) ~=~ P(56<I<60) ~=~ P(-0.4<Z<0.4) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big|<0.4\right)\]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(|Z|<0.4)\). I will also point out that \(\frac{2}{5} = 0.4\). In general, if \(X\) is normally distributed, then: \[P\left(\big|X-\mu\big|<d\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big| < \frac{d}{\sigma}\right)\]

From here we have a formula that lets us use the \(z\) table. (We practiced this part before.) \[\begin{aligned} P(|Z|<0.4) &= 2 \times P(Z<0.4)-1 \\ &= 2 \times 0.6554-1 \\ &= 0.3108 \end{aligned}\]
