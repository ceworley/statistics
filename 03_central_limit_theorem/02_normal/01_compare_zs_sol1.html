The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a measurement is the ratio with numerator the difference between measurement and population mean and denominator the population standard deviation.

\[z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \]

The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large measurement.

The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small measurement.

The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized measurement.

The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized measurement.


  1. We use the formula for calculating the lemon specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{123.1-115}{14} = 0.58 \]
  2. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.58) = 0.719 \]
  3. We use the formula for calculating the mango specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{233.1-246}{19} = -0.68 \]
  4. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-0.68) = 0.2483 \]
  5. We use the formula for calculating the watermelon specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{8598-9571}{700} = -1.39 \]
  6. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<-1.39) = 0.0823 \]
  7. We use the formula for calculating the apple specimen’s \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{225-220}{24} = 0.21 \]
  8. Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability \[P(Z<0.21) = 0.5832 \]
  9. We determine the maximum \(z\) score is \(0.58\), which belongs to the lemon. / . / . / .
  10. We determine the minimum \(z\) score is \(-1.39\), which belongs to the watermelon. / . / . / .
  11. We determine the minimum absolute \(z\) score is \(0.21\), which belongs to the apple. / . / . / .
  12. We determine the maximum absolute \(z\) score is \(1.39\), which belongs to the watermelon. / . / . / .
