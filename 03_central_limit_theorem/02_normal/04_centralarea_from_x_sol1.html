First, draw a sketch. We can label the \(K\) axis by adding integer multiples of 27 to 93. We know to shade the center because values near 93 satisfy the condition \(\big|K-93\big| < 27\). We draw the boundaries at \(93-27=66\) and \(93+27=120\) because those are the solutions to \(\big|K-93\big| = 27\). We can also rephrase the probability.

\[P\left(\big|K-93\big| < 27\right) ~=~ P(66<K<120)\]


We calculate the \(z\) values of the boundaries. Left boundary: \[\begin{aligned} z_1 &= \frac{66-93}{27} \\\\ &= \frac{-27}{27} \\\\ &= -1 \end{aligned}\]

Right boundary:

\[\begin{aligned} z_2 &= \frac{120-93}{27} \\\\ &= \frac{27}{27} \\\\ &= 1 \end{aligned}\]

We have rephrased our problem into a standard normal probability problem, because

\[P\left(\big|K-93\big| < 27\right) ~=~ P(66<K<120) ~=~ P(-1<Z<1) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big|<1\right)\]

So, we just need to evaluate \(P(|Z|<1)\). I will also point out that \(\frac{27}{27} = 1\). In general, if \(X\) is normally distributed, then: \[P\left(\big|X-\mu\big|<d\right) ~=~ P\left(\big|Z\big| < \frac{d}{\sigma}\right)\]

From here we have a formula that lets us use the \(z\) table. (We practiced this part before.) \[\begin{aligned} P(|Z|<1) &= 2 \times P(Z<1)-1 \\ &= 2 \times 0.8413-1 \\ &= 0.6826 \end{aligned}\]
