You may find it helpful to convert the densities to frequencies by multiplying each density by both the total sample size (\(n=200\)) and the width of the bar (2).


The first 4 questions involve adding up frequencies of the indicated intervals. The last 4 questions can be done by guessing and checking until something works.


  1. (ANS=0.855) There are 171 measurements less than 64. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<64]=0.855\).
  2. (ANS=0.895) There are 179 measurements more than 56. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>56]=0.895\).
  3. (ANS=0.2) There are 40 measurements closer than 1 units from 61. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-61|<1]=0.2\).
  4. (ANS=0.595) There are 119 measurements further than 2 units from 62. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-62|>2]=0.595\).
  5. (ANS=60) A boundary at \(b=60\) has 90 measurements less than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}<60]=0.45\).
  6. (ANS=62) A boundary at \(b=62\) has 70 measurements more than it. So, \(\text{prop}[\mathbf{x}>62]=0.35\).
  7. (ANS=2) An interval with radius \(r=2\) around center 62 contains 81 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-62|<2]=0.405\).
  8. (ANS=2) An interval with radius \(r=2\) around center 60 excludes 129 measurements. So, \(\text{prop}[|\mathbf{x}-60|>2]=0.645\).
