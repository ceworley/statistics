  1. Solution

    Use the definition of conditional probability. P(win given happy)=P(win and happy)P(happy)P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{P(\text{win and happy})}{P(\text{happy})} We can first determine all the joint probabilities. P(A and B)=P(A)⋅P(B given A)P(A\text{ and }B) = P(A)\cdot P(B\text{ given } A) P(win and happy)=0.3×0.8=0.24P(win and sad)=0.3×(1−0.8)=0.06P(lose and happy)=(1−0.3)×0.33=0.231P(lose and sad)=(1−0.3)×(1−0.33)=0.469\begin{aligned} P(\text{win and happy}) &= 0.3\times0.8 = 0.24\\ P(\text{win and sad}) &= 0.3\times(1-0.8) = 0.06\\ P(\text{lose and happy}) &= (1-0.3)\times0.33 = 0.231\\ P(\text{lose and sad}) &= (1-0.3)\times(1-0.33) = 0.469\\ \end{aligned}

    Notice there are two disjoint ways Bob could be happy. P(happy)=P([win and happy] OR [lose and happy])=P(win and happy)+P(lose and happy)=0.24+0.231=0.471\begin{aligned} P(\text{happy}) &= P\left(\text{[win and happy] OR [lose and happy]}\right) \\ &= P(\text{win and happy}) + P(\text{lose and happy}) \\ &= 0.24+0.231 \\ &= 0.471 \end{aligned} So, back to the conditional probability. P(win given happy)=0.240.471=0.51P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{0.24}{0.471} = 0.51
