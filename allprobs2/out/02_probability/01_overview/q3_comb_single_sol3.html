This scenario describes a combinations problem (order of selection does not matter). We are considering the subsets of size 4 from a set of size 12.

nCr=n!(n−r)!⋅r!n=12r=412C4=12!(12−4)!⋅4!=12!8!⋅4!=12⋅11⋅10⋅94⋅3⋅2⋅1=495\begin{aligned} {_nC_r} &= \frac{n!}{(n-r)! \cdot r!} \\\\ n &= 12 \\\\ r &= 4 \\\\ {_{12}C_{4}} &= \frac{12!}{(12-4)!\cdot 4! } \\\\ &= \frac{12!}{8! \cdot 4!} \\\\ &= \frac{ 12\cdot11\cdot10\cdot9}{ 4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot1} \\\\ &= 495 \end{aligned}

Remember, we care about combinations because they represent all the ways we can select rr 1s and n−rn-r 0s. So, in this case, 12C4=495{_{12}C_{4}} = 495 tells us there are 495 ways of selecting 8 0s and 4 1s. (Think of the 1s as the toppings that are selected and 0s as the toppings NOT selected.)
